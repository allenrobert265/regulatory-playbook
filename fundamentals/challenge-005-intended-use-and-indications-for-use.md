# 📖 Introduction

One of the first questions a Regulatory Affairs professional asks about any medical device is:

> **"What is the intended use of this device?"**

The answer determines how the device may be regulated.

The intended use influences:

* Device classification
* Applicable regulations
* Required testing
* Clinical evidence
* Risk management
* Labeling
* Regulatory submission pathway

Even if two devices look identical, they may follow completely different regulatory pathways if their intended uses are different.

---

# 📘 What is Intended Use?

**Intended Use** describes the **medical purpose** of a device as defined by the manufacturer.

It explains:

* What the device is intended to do.
* The purpose of the device.
* The medical function it performs.

The Intended Use should be clear, objective, and consistent across:

* Product labeling
* Instructions for Use (IFU)
* Marketing materials
* Regulatory submissions
* Technical documentation

---

## Example

### Device

Digital Blood Pressure Monitor

### Intended Use

> The device is intended to non-invasively measure systolic and diastolic blood pressure and pulse rate in adults.

Notice that this statement focuses only on **what the device is intended to do**.

---

# 📘 What are Indications for Use?

**Indications for Use** provide more detailed information about:

* Who should use the device.
* Which patients the device is intended for.
* Under what conditions it should be used.
* The clinical setting in which it is used.

Think of it this way:

| Question                 | Answered By         |
| ------------------------ | ------------------- |
| What does the device do? | Intended Use        |
| Who uses it?             | Indications for Use |
| When is it used?         | Indications for Use |
| Where is it used?        | Indications for Use |

---

## Example

### Intended Use

Measures blood pressure.

### Indications for Use

Intended for adults over 18 years of age for non-invasive blood pressure monitoring in home healthcare settings.

---

# 🔍 Intended Use vs Indications for Use

| Intended Use                      | Indications for Use                                   |
| --------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| Describes the medical purpose     | Describes the patient population and clinical setting |
| Focuses on *what* the device does | Focuses on *who*, *when*, and *where*                 |
| Usually broader                   | Usually more detailed                                 |

---

# 📌 Why is Intended Use Important?

The Intended Use determines many regulatory decisions.

It helps determine:

* Whether the product is a medical device.
* Device classification.
* Applicable regulations.
* Required standards.
* Testing requirements.
* Clinical evidence.
* Regulatory submission pathway.

Without a clearly defined Intended Use, it is difficult to develop an appropriate regulatory strategy.

# ⚠️ Marketing Claims Matter

Consider two smartwatches.

### Smartwatch A

Claim:

> Tracks daily steps and physical activity.

This is generally considered a consumer wellness product.

---

### Smartwatch B

Claim:

> Detects atrial fibrillation and alerts users to seek medical attention.

This is making a medical claim and may therefore be regulated as a medical device.

The hardware may be identical.

The regulatory pathway changes because the **Intended Use has changed.**

---

# ❌ Poor Intended Use Statement

> Our revolutionary AI-powered device is the world's most accurate blood pressure monitor.

Problems:

* Promotional language
* Subjective claims
* No clear medical purpose

---

# ✅ Better Intended Use Statement

> Intended to non-invasively measure blood pressure and pulse rate in adults.

Characteristics:

* Clear
* Objective
* Specific
* Professional
* Regulatory appropriate

---

# 💼 Role of Regulatory Affairs

Regulatory Affairs professionals review Intended Use statements because they influence:

* Device classification
* Applicable regulations
* Risk management
* Clinical evaluation
* Labeling
* Marketing claims
* Regulatory submissions

A poorly written Intended Use statement can result in delays, additional regulatory questions, or an incorrect regulatory pathway.

---

# 💡 Key Takeaways

* Intended Use defines the medical purpose of a device.
* Indications for Use describe who should use the device and under what conditions.
* Intended Use influences almost every regulatory decision.
* Marketing claims should be accurate and supported by evidence.
* A well-written Intended Use statement should be clear, objective, and consistent.
