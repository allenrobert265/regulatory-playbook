# 📖 Introduction

A medical device does not begin when it is manufactured.

It begins with an idea.

From that idea, the device goes through research, design, testing, regulatory approval, manufacturing, marketing, and continuous monitoring after it reaches patients.

This complete journey is known as the **Medical Device Lifecycle**.

Regulatory Affairs is involved throughout the lifecycle to ensure the device remains safe, effective, and compliant with applicable regulations.

# 📌 Stage-by-Stage Explanation

## 1. Need Identified

Everything starts with a problem.

Example:

Patients need a faster way to monitor blood glucose without frequent finger pricks.

The company identifies an unmet clinical need.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Understand intended use.
- Determine whether the product will be regulated as a medical device.

---

## 2. Concept & Idea

Engineers brainstorm possible solutions.

Example:

A wearable glucose monitoring patch.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Provide early regulatory guidance.
- Identify similar products already on the market.

---

## 3. Research & Feasibility

The concept is evaluated.

Questions include:

- Is it technically possible?
- Is there a clinical need?
- Is it commercially viable?

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Identify applicable regulations.
- Determine device classification.
- Identify required standards.

---

## 4. Design & Development

The product is designed and prototypes are developed.

Activities include:

- Hardware development
- Software development
- Design reviews
- Prototype testing

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Ensure design controls are followed.
- Support documentation.
- Ensure compliance with applicable standards.

---

## 5. Risk Management

Potential hazards are identified.

Questions include:

- What could go wrong?
- How serious is the harm?
- How can risks be reduced?

Most medical device manufacturers follow **ISO 14971** for risk management.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Ensure risk management activities meet regulatory expectations.
- Maintain risk documentation.

---

## 6. Verification & Validation

The device is tested.

Verification asks:

> Did we build the device correctly?

Validation asks:

> Did we build the correct device?

Examples include:

- Electrical safety testing
- Software testing
- Biocompatibility
- Performance testing

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Ensure evidence supports regulatory submissions.
- Verify required testing has been completed.

---

## 7. Regulatory Submission

The company prepares documents required for regulatory approval.

Depending on the country, submissions may go to:

- CDSCO
- FDA
- EU Notified Bodies

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Prepare submission documents.
- Communicate with regulatory authorities.
- Respond to regulatory questions.

---

## 8. Market Approval

After review, the product may receive authorization for sale.

Only then can it legally enter the market.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Maintain approvals.
- Track regulatory commitments.

---

## 9. Manufacturing

The approved device enters production.

Manufacturing must follow a Quality Management System.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Ensure manufacturing remains compliant.
- Support inspections and audits.

---

## 10. Distribution

The product is supplied to hospitals, clinics, distributors, and patients.

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Ensure labeling remains compliant.
- Manage regulatory changes across markets.

---

## 11. Post-Market Surveillance

Regulatory work does not stop after product launch.

Manufacturers monitor:

- Complaints
- Device failures
- Adverse events
- Customer feedback
- Product recalls

### Regulatory Affairs Role

- Monitor product performance.
- Report adverse events when required.
- Support CAPA.
- Support recalls if necessary.

---

## 12. Product Improvement

Based on real-world experience, manufacturers improve the product.

Changes may require:

- Updated documentation
- Additional testing
- New regulatory submissions

Regulatory Affairs ensures changes remain compliant.

# 💡 Key Takeaways

- Regulatory Affairs begins at the idea stage—not at submission.
- Compliance is a continuous process.
- Every lifecycle stage generates documentation.
- Patient safety remains the primary objective throughout the lifecycle.
